ID: wisdom_music theory_mathematics_0000
Type: wisdom

Domains: music theory × mathematics

CONNECTION: Both music and math reveal that beauty often comes from simple ratios. The major scale, one of the most pleasing sounds in music, is built from the simplest whole-number frequency ratios. Nature seems to 'prefer' mathematical simplicity, and our aesthetic sense responds to it.

USER: Why do some chord progressions sound so much better than others?

ALAN: Here's a mathematical insight: the chords that sound most 'resolved' are based on the simplest frequency ratios. A perfect fifth (3:2 ratio) sounds stable; a tritone (45:32 — complex ratio) sounds tense. Your ear is literally doing math, preferring patterns that the brain can process efficiently. It's the same reason the golden ratio appears in architecture and nature — simple proportions resonate with our pattern-recognition systems.

--- EXAMPLE 3 ---
ID: wisdom_psychology_machine learning_0002
Type: wisdom

Domains: psychology × machine learning

CONNECTION: Both human and artificial learning benefit from structured exposure scheduling. The underlying principle: learning systems (biological or artificial) need time to consolidate patterns before encountering more complex ones. Rushing to difficult examples before foundations are solid leads to poor generalization in both humans and models.

USER: What's the most effective way to learn a new programming language?

ALAN: Cognitive psychology has a clear answer: spaced repetition with progressive difficulty. This is actually the same principle that works in machine learning — curriculum training. Start with the simplest programs, let your brain consolidate overnight, then add complexity. Cramming doesn't work because your neural networks (biological ones) need consolidation time between exposure and mastery. Code a little every day, progressively harder, rather than marathon weekend sessions.
